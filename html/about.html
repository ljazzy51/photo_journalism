Why the Queer Experiences of New York City?

Growing Up

I grew up in a quiet suburban town. I went to church on Sundays, church youth group on Wednesdays, and sports after school. Whatever "free time" I had was spent at school, work, volunteering, or doing homework. I was expected to do well in school but better in life. The wildest thing that happened on a weeknight was sitting in the CVS parking lot talking about essentially made up drama. It wouldn't matter if I was getting straight As or B+s in school. If I was not supporting my community, I would not be judged as a good person. Unfortunately, the standards for "good person" for my parents were very different from what I thought of for myself. While I placed priorities on society and community based programs, I was always told that I should "invest in my future" I was told that I should care about my future and my career. I was also told that if I came out or showed any of my personality that I would either get fired or never get hired in the first place. I was told that my queer identity would be the final and overriding card of my career. I would never hide my identity again because I refuse to send the message that queer people do not have the same options for careers that queer people do


Queer Life

When I was 12, my mom read my texts with the first girlfriend I ever had. It turned into a whole thing. The only thing I gained from those years was that I needed to be sneakier and that I needed to give up everything I did that could connect me to queer culture. I hid a crucial part of my identity for 5 years. When I was in Junior year of high school I got the opportunity of a lifetime. I got into a social activism program that would allow me to spend my entire Junior year in New York City. When I got here my small suburban world exploded and I was able to express a whole new side of myself. I got to experience parts of queer culture I didnt even know existed. Unfortunately, my Junior year happened to be in 2020. Due to the pandemic, I had to cut my experience short return to my small suburban world. The difference when I returned was that I was not willing to give up everything I experienced in New York, everything I got to be. This exposure to New York queer culture allowed me to find myself and it meant everything to me. I owe who I am now to queer culture and I know I am not alone in that.
